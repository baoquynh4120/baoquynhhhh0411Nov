9 Types Of Nutritious Bushfood In Australia Nature

  1. Quandong

    2. Binomial name: Santalum acuminatum
    Description
    Known as one of the most versatile bush fruits in Australia as it can be used in cook and medicine. Quandong trees normally grow throughout desserts of Australia (arid or semi-arid areas), can reach up to 4m height. Their flowers are formed as cup shape in late summer and fructify in early spring.
    Usage
    Quandong flesh is consumed in pies and jams, high in nutrients (large amount of vitamin C). The kernel is mostly used for medicine purposes. Also, Aborigines use the Quandong wood in making traditional bowls (pitti or coolamons).

  2. Lemon Myrtle

    3. Binomial name: Backhousia citriodora
    Description
    Considered as Queen of the Lemon Herbs. It is popularly used because of the intense citrus fragrance and flavour from the leaves. Lemon Myrtle is harvested all year, grown mostly in the central of subtropical rainforest or south-eastern Queensland; can be up to 3-8m tall with oval glossy green leaves in 5-12cm length.
    Usage
    Lemon Myrtle leaves are applied in cook (cakes, sauces, syrups, tea, etc.) and has been used in Aboriginal cuisine or medicine for long. People also produce the essential oil by distilling Myrtle leaves.

  3. Finger Lemon

    4. Binomial name: Citrus australasica
    Description
    It is known as a gourmet bushfood. Finger lime takes from 4-6 years to bear flowers and then fruits, can grow at 1-3m height or up to 6m if unpruned. The fruits are harvested normally around November to March.
    Usage
    Finger Lime has tangy and sour flavour, the juice is acidic and tastes the same as normal lime. People use spherical juice vesicles (also called pearls) from this lemon for garnish or most of dishes. Finger lemon peel can be dried to use for flavouring spice.

  4. Pepperberry

    5. Binomial name: Tasmannia lanceolata
    Description
    Mostly found in cool temperature rainforest, Tasmannia, northwards through Victoria to Barrington Tops in NSW. Pepperberry is grown as shrubs, normally reach to 2-10m height with light red stems. Its aromatic leaves are 0.7-2cm wide and 4-12cm long; bearing flowers in summer and fructify in autumn.
    Usage
    Tasmannia lanceolata is mostly used by its leaves and berry as a spice with peppery taste; added into food and dishes or either used in colonial medicine.

  5. Copperleaf Snowberry

    6. Binomial name: Gaultheria hispida
    Description
    It is exclusively grown in Tasmania or some parts in Victoria; can be found in rainforest, cool, moist or alpine areas. Gaultheria hispida can grow up to 2m in good condition with multi erect branches. Its leaves are long glossy dark green with tinged copper margins; length is 4-9cm.
    Usage
    This kind of snowberry is edible, contains bitter taste. It is mostly collected by Aboriginal people as bushfood, eaten as either cook or raw fruit.

  6. Kakadu Plum

    7. Binomial name: Terminalia ferdinandiana
    Description
    It is grown as a slender tree in tropical woodlands from northwestern Australia to eastern Arnhem Land; can be up to 14m height with deciduous pale green leaves. Bearing small, perfumed flowers with creamy white colour from September to December. The fruit is yellow-green with almond shape and one large seed inside.
    Usage
    Kakadu plum is eaten raw or made as jam; collected by Aboriginal people to use as bush tucker or traditional medicine.

  7. Lemon Aspen

    8. Binomial name: Acronychia superba
    Description
    It is widespread in Queensland or rainforests. Its height can be up 27m with elliptical leaves, small white flowers and spherical fruit.
    Usage
    Lemon Aspen is collected as bushfood, has grapefruit and lime-like flavour. People use it mostly for sauces, beverages and confectionary.

  8. Riberry

    9. Binomial name: Syzygium luehmannii
    Description
    Riberri is also called lilly pilly or cherry alder. Typically growing up to 8-10m height with pinky red leaves (when it is young) and shiny green leaves (when mature). Its flowers form in November to December as cream colour. Fructifying as pear-shaped red berry from December to February.
    Usage
    Riberry is popularly used for jam, sauces, confectionary and syrups or in raw. The plant can also be grown as ornamental tree.

  9. Muntries

    10. Binomial name: Kunzea pomifera
    Description
    . Muntries require well drained or acidic soil to cultivate; mainly found in Victoria and South Australia. It is grown as low shrubs with hairy stems; leaves are egg-shaped with creamy flowers. The fruit is spherical, deep red or purple when mature.
    Usage
    Muntries are eaten raw; have flavour of spicy apple and contain 4 times more antioxidants than blueberries. It is also used as natural waxes which is good for skin.
